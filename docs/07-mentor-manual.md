# Mentor Manual

Mentors help teams think clearly, verify claims, and ship a working demo. Mentors are not there to take over, rewrite the project, or turn a sprint into a lecture.

## What Mentors Are For

- Sharpen the user and problem.
- Reduce scope until the team can demo.
- Spot hidden risk, missing data, and weak claims.
- Help teams use AI tools with better prompts and better review.
- Ask for evidence.
- Keep non-programmers included.
- Push toward a working prototype with honest limits.

## What Mentors Are Not For

- Owning the repo.
- Writing the whole prototype.
- Replacing team decisions.
- Selling a tool.
- Turning feedback into a status contest.
- Pressuring teams toward sponsor or administration needs.

## The 10-Minute Feedback Structure

Minute 1: ask the team to show the current demo path, not slides.

Minutes 2-3: ask who the named user is and what pain the prototype reduces.

Minutes 4-5: inspect what works right now.

Minutes 6-7: name the biggest risk or confusion.

Minutes 8-9: agree one concrete next action.

Minute 10: write the action in the team's build log.

## Good Questions

- Who has this problem by name or role?
- What can you show live in 5 minutes?
- What did AI generate that you have not verified?
- What source proves this answer?
- What will you cut if you have two hours left?
- What part must be human-reviewed?
- What would make this dangerous if people believed it too much?

## Behaviors To Avoid

- Taking the keyboard without permission.
- Saying "just build X" without checking team capacity.
- Adding features after midpoint.
- Treating civic context as decoration.
- Letting confident AI output pass as verified fact.
- Making non-programmers feel like spectators.

## Helping Non-Programmers

Give non-programmers ownership of user testing, source verification, prompt design, data correction, demo script, documentation, and public explanation. These are core build tasks, not side work.

Pair them with technical teammates in short loops: ask, generate, inspect, correct, document.

## Unblocking Without Solving

Offer options, not commands. Help teams choose the smallest path to a demo. If you write code, do it as a teaching moment and hand control back quickly.

## Spotting AI Hallucinations

Check citations, dates, numbers, legal claims, procurement claims, and named entities. Ask the model to list factual claims and support each claim from provided sources. Then manually inspect a sample.

Any unsupported claim must be removed, marked uncertain, or replaced with a source-linked answer.

## Push Toward Functional Demo

A functional demo beats a polished story. The prototype can be narrow, ugly, and honest. It must do one useful thing in front of the room.
