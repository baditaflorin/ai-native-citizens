# Organizer Playbook

AI-Native Citizens sprints are local civic AI build sprints. Teams work on real public problems, use AI-native workflows across the whole build process, publish mandatory build logs, and demo working prototypes with clear limits.

## Sprint Format

- Duration: 48 to 72 hours.
- Team size: 3 to 5 people.
- Challenge count: minimum 5 for Official Local Sprint.
- Outputs: prototype, demo, repository or shareable artifact, build log.
- Judging: global rubric in `docs/08-judge-rubric.md`.
- Public demo: finalists pitch; all teams get feedback.

## Six-Week Timeline

Week 1: lock local lead, co-lead, venue target, date, event tier, and minimum budget.

Week 2: recruit mentors, judges, MC, ops lead, photo/video person, and challenge owners.

Week 3: publish event page, open participant registration, announce first 5 challenges, confirm municipality as partner-of-record if relevant.

Week 4: run mentor briefing, challenge review, accessibility check, venue walkthrough, and communications push.

Week 5: send participant guide, confirm food, power, screens, Wi-Fi, badges, signage, and demo-day timing.

Week 6: run sprint, collect build logs, publish projects, send thank-you notes, and schedule 30-day follow-up.

## Roles

Local lead: owns the event, partner relationships, venue, budget, and final calls.

Co-lead: shadows the lead, handles continuity, and can run the event if the lead is unavailable.

Ops lead: registration, food, badges, signage, rooms, timekeeping, and accessibility.

Mentors: help teams think, verify, unblock, and ship. They do not take over.

Judges: score using the global rubric verbatim.

MC: keeps demo day sharp, fair, and on time.

Photo/video: documents teams, demos, judges, organizers, and public room energy.

## Minimum Viable Budget

- Venue: donated preferred.
- Food and drinks: enough for two working days.
- Printed badges and signage: cheap, readable, useful.
- Prize pool: optional; micro-grants are better than trophies.
- Mentor travel: optional but useful for regional events.
- Photo/video: one person with clear shot list.
- Accessibility reserve: captions, transport support, childcare support, or other local needs.

## Logistics Checklist

- Venue with late access and clear closing rules.
- Stable Wi-Fi with guest network and posted password.
- Extension cords, power strips, and safe cable routes.
- Projector or large screen for demos.
- Separate judge room or quiet scoring area.
- Food that does not require complicated service.
- Water, coffee, tea, and clear waste bins.
- Badges for participants, mentors, judges, organizers, press, and partners.
- Wayfinding signs, schedule posters, and challenge wall.
- Photo/video consent note at registration.

## Recruiting Participants and Mentors

Recruit mixed teams: students, civic workers, journalists, designers, developers, NGO staff, data people, parents, and residents who know the problem. Do not make the room developer-only.

Mentors should cover product thinking, civic context, design, engineering, data, accessibility, public administration, and responsible AI verification.

## Working With Municipalities

Universities and neutral civic NGOs are preferred hosts. Municipalities are partners-of-record, challenge sources, data providers, demo-day guests, and continuation partners.

This keeps the movement non-partisan and protects teams from becoming unpaid delivery arms for public administration.

## Anti-Exploitation Posture

Projects belong to teams.

Open-source is the default, with clear license.

No sprint prototype is promised as production-ready software to any administration.

Administrations do not take team work without explicit consent.

Continuation requires explicit acknowledgment, credit, micro-grant, or contract.

Participants receive education, visibility, feedback, network, and real ownership.

## Selecting Challenges

Use problems that are local, observable, and demoable in 48 to 72 hours. Avoid challenges that require private data, procurement access, production integrations, or policy authority to show value.

Good challenge sources: public budgets, council decisions, service pages, PDF archives, procurement portals, school rules, NGO admin pain, local journalism research, and open-data gaps.

## Demo Day Ops

All teams submit build logs before demo. Organizers select finalists using mentor input and prototype readiness. Finalists pitch publicly. Non-finalists still get mentor/judge feedback and project listing.

Demo rules: 5 minutes demo, 3 minutes questions, real interaction preferred, no video-only demos unless the prototype cannot run live for access reasons.

## 30 Days After

Publish all projects and build logs within 7 days. Run follow-up calls at days 7, 14, and 30. Match promising teams with maintainers, micro-grants, pilot partners, or honest closure.

Continuation is work. Assign an owner before the sprint starts.
